# ENG 1005 · Grammar & Mechanics Study Guide
### Writing About Mystery and Thriller

Use this guide to prepare for the Sentence Structure, Grammar, and Mechanics Exam.
Each section explains a concept, shows a mystery-themed example, points out the
mistake students make most often, and gives you a quick test you can use on your
own writing. Work through it with a pen — try fixing each "✗" sentence yourself
before reading the "✓".

---

## A. Independent vs. Subordinate Clauses

- An **independent clause (IC)** expresses a complete thought and can stand alone
  as a sentence.
  - ✓ *The butler had vanished before midnight.*
- A **subordinate (dependent) clause (SC)** has a subject and verb but cannot
  stand alone. It usually begins with a subordinating word such as *because,
  although, when, if, since, while, after, before, that, which.*
  - ✗ *Although the alibi seemed airtight* — leaves you waiting for the rest.

**Quick test:** Read the clause by itself. If it feels unfinished ("…and then
what?"), it's subordinate.

**Watch for:** words like *when, because, although, since* at the start — they
almost always signal a subordinate clause.

---

## B. Sentence Fragments

A **fragment** is a piece of a sentence punctuated as if it were whole. It's
usually missing a subject, a verb, or a complete thought.

- ✗ *Hiding in the shadows near the conservatory.* (no subject + verb that make a complete thought)
- ✓ *The killer was hiding in the shadows near the conservatory.*
- ✗ *Because no one had heard the gunshot.* (subordinate clause left alone)
- ✓ *Because no one had heard the gunshot, the body lay undiscovered for hours.*

**Quick test:** Can the word group stand alone and make complete sense? If not,
attach it to a nearby sentence or add what's missing.

**Watch for:** groups beginning with *-ing* words (*Hiding…*), or with *which,
who, because, although* standing alone.

---

## C. Comma Splices & Fused (Run-on) Sentences

Both errors jam two independent clauses together incorrectly.

- **Comma splice (CS):** two complete sentences joined with only a comma.
  - ✗ *The lights went out, no one screamed.*
- **Fused / run-on (FS):** two complete sentences with no punctuation at all.
  - ✗ *The clock struck twelve the body was discovered.*

**Five ways to fix either one:**
1. Period: *The lights went out. No one screamed.*
2. Semicolon: *The lights went out; no one screamed.*
3. Comma **+** coordinating conjunction (*and, but, or, so, for, nor, yet*):
   *The lights went out, but no one screamed.*
4. Subordinate one clause: *When the clock struck twelve, the body was discovered.*
5. Rewrite as one idea.

**Quick test:** If you can put a period where the comma is (or where the gap is)
and get two real sentences, you have a splice or run-on — fix it.

## D. Commas

The most common comma rules tested:

1. **After an introductory element:**
   - ✓ *After the storm passed, the investigators returned to the cliff house.*
2. **Between items in a series** (include the comma before *and* — the Oxford comma):
   - ✓ *The maid found a torn ticket, a broken watch, and a bloody glove.*
3. **Around non-essential information** (use a pair of commas):
   - ✓ *Agatha Christie, who wrote* And Then There Were None*, mastered the locked-room puzzle.*
4. **After an introductory** *Yes/No* **or a name in direct address:**
   - ✓ *Yes, the safe had been opened from the inside.*
5. **Between coordinate adjectives:**
   - ✓ *The suspect, tall, pale, and nervous, avoided eye contact.*

**Watch for:** don't drop a comma between subject and verb randomly, and don't
forget the *second* comma when information is set off in the middle.

## E. Semicolons & Colons

- **Semicolon ( ; )** joins two **closely related complete sentences**:
  - ✓ *The evidence was overwhelming; the jury deliberated for only an hour.*
  - Also use it before a **conjunctive adverb** (*however, therefore, moreover*)
    that joins two sentences: *The first clue was subtle; however, no one noticed it.*
- **Colon ( : )** introduces a **list, explanation, or statement** after a
  complete sentence:
  - ✓ *Only three suspects remained: the cook, the chauffeur, and the niece.*
  - ✓ *The detective had one rule: never trust a calm witness.*

**Quick test:** A colon needs a *complete sentence before it*. A semicolon needs
a *complete sentence on both sides*.

---

## F. Coordination & Subordination (Combining Sentences)

Combine short, choppy sentences to show how ideas relate.

- **Coordination** links equal ideas with a comma + *and, but, or, so, yet*:
  - *The alarm failed, **so** the intruder slipped inside.*
- **Subordination** makes one idea depend on another using *although, because,
  when, since, while, which*:
  - *Although the window was locked, the intruder still got in.*
  - *The letter, **which** arrived on Monday, changed the entire case.*

**Tip:** choose the connector that fits the meaning — *because* shows cause,
*although* shows contrast, *when* shows time.

---

## G. Parallelism

Items in a list or pair should share the **same grammatical form**.

- ✗ *The detective was clever, observant, and acted with caution.*
  (two adjectives + a verb phrase — not parallel)
- ✓ *The detective was clever, observant, and cautious.* (three adjectives)
- ✗ *She liked solving puzzles, to question suspects, and the thrill of the chase.*
- ✓ *She liked solving puzzles, questioning suspects, and chasing leads.* (three *-ing* forms)

**With paired connectors** (*not only… but also*, *either… or*), keep both halves
parallel:
- ✓ *He not only forged the will but also hid the body.*

**Quick test:** line the items up. Do they all start with the same kind of word
(all *-ing*, all nouns, all adjectives)?

---

## H. Misplaced & Dangling Modifiers

A modifier should sit **next to the word it describes**.

- **Dangling:** the thing being described isn't actually in the sentence.
  - ✗ *Covered in dust, the detective found the old diary.* (the detective is covered in dust?)
  - ✓ *The detective found the old diary, which was covered in dust.*
  - ✗ *Running down the corridor, the lights suddenly went out.*
  - ✓ *As she ran down the corridor, the lights suddenly went out.*
- **Misplaced:** the modifier is in the wrong spot, changing the meaning.
  - ✗ *The witness described the suspect who fled in a trembling voice.*
  - ✓ *In a trembling voice, the witness described the suspect who fled.*
  - ✗ *We almost solved every clue.* (we solved none?)
  - ✓ *We solved almost every clue.*

**Quick test:** ask *"Who or what is doing this action?"* Put that word right next
to the modifying phrase.

## I. Subject–Verb Agreement & Pronoun Case

**Agreement** — the verb must match the subject:
- *Each, every, everyone, neither, either* are **singular**:
  - ✓ *Each of the suspects **has** an alibi.*  ✓ *Everyone must account for **his or her** whereabouts.*
- With *neither… nor / either… or*, the verb agrees with the **nearer** subject:
  - ✓ *Neither the maid nor the twins **were** in the room.*
- Collective nouns (*jury, team, committee*) are usually **singular**:
  - ✓ *The jury reached **its** verdict.*

**Pronoun case** — *I/he/she/we/they* are subjects; *me/him/her/us/them* are objects:
- After a preposition, use the object form:
  - ✓ *Between you and **me**, the butler is lying.*
  - ✓ *The detective handed the file to Watson and **me**.*
- **Trick:** drop the other person and test it — "handed the file to *me*" (not "to *I*").

---

## J. Editing a Passage

On the exam you'll fix a paragraph containing several mixed errors. Read it once
for meaning, then hunt for each error type in turn:

1. **Comma splices / run-ons** — two sentences fused or comma-joined.
2. **Fragments** — especially groups starting with *which* or *-ing*.
3. **Missing commas** — introductory phrases and series.
4. **Agreement** — *each / everyone* with a plural verb.
5. **Dangling modifiers** — an opening phrase with the wrong subject after it.

**Strategy:** fix one error type per pass. It's easier to catch every comma
splice in one read-through than to catch everything at once.
